Bias and consistency

Roughly, estimators are described by two sets of orthogonal adjectives:

  1. Unbiased: the bias of the estimator, B(Θ^)B(\hat\Theta)B(Θ^), equals 000.
  2. Consistent: for any ϵ>0\epsilon>0ϵ>0, we have

    lim⁡n→∞P(∣Θ^n−θ∣≥ϵ)=0.\lim_{n\to\infty}P(|\hat\Theta_n-\theta|\geq\epsilon)=0. n→∞lim​P(∣Θ^n​−θ∣≥ϵ)=0.

    That is, with more samples, the estimators get arbitrarily close to the true value.

Unbiased and consistent (a good estimator): We simply choose the sample mean Xˉ=(X1+⋯ )/n\bar X=(X_1+\cdots)/nXˉ=(X1​+⋯)/n.

Unbiased and inconsistent: An unbiased estimator is easy to construct: simply a distribution spread 50% at 000, and 50% at 111 will do. For any ϵ<0.5\epsilon<0.5ϵ<0.5, the probability that anything estimated will be greater than that epsilon is exactly 111, so it is inconsistent.

Then, the mean of the estimator distribution is proportional to the mean for the Beta distribution, at n/(n+1)⋅θn/(n+1)\cdot\thetan/(n+1)⋅θ. Alternatively, the true parameter θ\thetaθ must obviously be greater than the nnn-th order statistic, so estimating exactly the that order statistic is almost certainly an underestimate, should there be any probability mass anywehre else.

This approach comes arbitrarily close to the true parameter, however, and for that we need two facts:

  1. The mean, E[Θ^]=nn+1θE[\hat\Theta]=\frac{n}{n+1}\thetaE[Θ^]=n+1n​θ gets arbitrarily close to θ\thetaθ.

Hence, it is true that Θ^\hat\ThetaΘ^ converges in probability to θ\thetaθ, and indeed, this estimator is consistent.

Intuitively, unbiased-ness is quite strong—it requires that for any nnn number of samples, the estimator be unbiased, while consistency only requires convergence as n→∞n\to\inftyn→∞.

Furthermore, notably, the convergence of the MSE of the estimator to 000 as n→∞n\to\inftyn→∞ also proves consistency. This is because the bias and the variance both must go to 000 (speed irrelevant). This is nearly a tautology.[2]
